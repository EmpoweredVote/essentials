## Topic 1: judicial-criminal-justice
**Topic ID:** `9db07b16-1076-4b7d-ad89-ebe7b51f4336`
**Title:** Criminal Justice Approach

### Selected Value: 2

**Stance description for value 2:**
"Giving the person a fair chance to make things right — through treatment, community service, or restitution."

### Reasoning:
Ashouri explicitly frames the criminal justice role of the City Attorney as rehabilitation and reform over punishment. In her Patch profile Q&A, she stated: "As city attorney, I would reform the criminal division and train attorneys to recognize cases where constitutional rights are violated and to not file those cases. My goal would be to abolish racial profiling in my office." She describes wanting to "allow for changes in sentencing for non-violent cases to allow for people to seek rehabilitation and schooling" and that she would "make a policy not to criminalize poverty, and to focus resources on violent crimes." Her platform page states she wants to "focus on drug rehabilitation and providing services like housing rather than criminalizing addiction or poverty." She explicitly supports diversion and restorative justice ("prioritizing mediation and restorative justice in our communities" on aida4la.com), and describes her goal as "to assist the survivors" and "employ more social workers to provide support." This positions her clearly at value 2: giving people a fair chance through treatment and community service. She does not go to value 1 (pure rehabilitation) because she does acknowledge serious/violent crimes must be prosecuted ("focus resources on violent crimes") and explicitly notes vehicular crimes and gun cases deserve prosecution. Value 2 best matches her position: fair chance through treatment/rehabilitation, reserving prosecution for cases that genuinely harm people.

## Topic 2: judicial-access-to-justice
**Topic ID:** `9d45acaf-1ba4-4cb8-95e1-5ed985223b91`
**Title:** Access to Justice

### Selected Value: 1

**Stance description for value 1:**
"Easy. Courts exist for everyone — not just people with expensive lawyers. Low barriers mean more access to justice."

### Reasoning:
Ashouri's entire career and platform is organized around eliminating barriers to legal access for low-income residents, renters, immigrants, and workers. The LAist voter guide notes her "top priorities include supporting renters and defending tenants from landlords" and she frames the City Attorney's Office as a vehicle for championing those who cannot afford expensive legal representation. Her platform commits to reopening the Code Enforcement Unit to go after landlords refusing to do repairs — framing government legal resources as a tool directly for low-income residents. She was a staff attorney at Legal Aid Foundation of Los Angeles and Public Counsel, both free legal service organizations. Her Patch Q&A explicitly contrasts her approach with the incumbent's "office catering to real estate interests" and describes creating a department to assist survivors and "publicizing information in multiple languages" and "creating a hotline for people to report their claims available in multiple languages, we can empower workers and make the office more accessible." The pattern — free legal aid work, multilingual outreach, reopening tenant enforcement units, explicit rejection of "expensive outsourcing to big law firms" — best matches value 1: courts and legal resources exist for everyone, not just those with expensive lawyers; low barriers mean more access to justice.

## Topic 3: judicial-government-deference
**Topic ID:** `e5e48f0e-8f3a-40e1-8080-889fea389603`
**Title:** Judicial & Prosecutorial Discretion

### Selected Value: 1

**Stance description for value 1:**
"The citizen, almost always. Government has lawyers, money, and power. Regular people need courts to level the playing field."

### Reasoning:
For City Attorney candidates, this topic captures whether the candidate sees the role as reflexively defending city decisions and employees, or exercising independent judgment to protect residents against city overreach. Ashouri's entire platform is built on the premise that the City Attorney's Office should serve ordinary residents against powerful institutions — including city government itself. The LAist voter guide quotes: "We need new leadership that has the right experience, knowledge, and passion that will keep the City Council accountable." Her Patch profile states the incumbent created an "office catering to real estate interests" while she will hold "slumlords accountable" and reopen the Code Enforcement Unit to go after bad landlords. She explicitly says "the city should be held accountable for its negligence" regarding liability payouts, and frames her role as keeping the City Council accountable (not defending it). Her civil rights and legal aid background — representing immigrants, low-income renters, workers — directly embodies the value 1 framing: government has lawyers, money, and power; the City Attorney should help regular people. The AOL/LA Times piece reinforces this: she describes concluding as a former deputy city attorney that "too many minor cases were heading to trial" and the office needed reform to stop filing cases driven by government interest rather than justice. Inferred from overall platform framing — no single explicit quote on the "citizen vs. government" question, but the consistent direction across all sources is unambiguous: citizen-favoring over reflexive city defense.

## Topic 4: judicial-transparency
**Topic ID:** `6674d87e-999d-433a-aab7-3f626f59fd5f`
**Title:** Transparency in Legal Proceedings

### Selected Value: 2

**Stance description for value 2:**
"Default to open proceedings. Sealing records or closing hearings requires a compelling, documented reason."

### Reasoning:
Ashouri consistently emphasizes transparency and accountability as core values, but her statements focus on office operations and accountability to the public rather than detailed positions on court record sealing or hearing openness. The LAist voter guide reports she pledges to make the City Attorney's Office "accountable to the people of Los Angeles" by "restructuring the office, eliminating expensive outsourcing of legal work to big law firms and creating focused units." Her Patch Q&A discusses using social media "to make the office more transparent and accountable" and describes her city as having "a terrible legacy of corruption and opaqueness, which disenfranchises the community." Her campaign website lists "accountability and transparency" prominently. These statements support a strong pro-transparency default stance (value 2: default to open, sealing requires compelling reason), but she does not make explicit statements that everything possible must be public at the cost of all other interests (value 1: secrecy breeds injustice). Her civil-rights legal background — working on behalf of tenants and immigrants who need access to legal information — supports a strong default-open stance. Value 2 best matches: default to openness, with procedural sealing reserved for compelling reasons (consistent with her legal services background where clients need transparent processes to protect their rights). Inferred from general transparency framing and civil rights background — no specific quote on court record transparency per se.

## Topic 5: judicial-police-accountability
**Topic ID:** `7bad33eb-e93e-4d94-8822-97212d49bde5`
**Title:** Police Accountability

### Selected Value: 1

**Stance description for value 1:**
"Investigate independently. The office works for the public — not the officials it's supposed to keep accountable."

### Reasoning:
Ashouri has explicit, specific positions on police accountability that go beyond general statements. The Patch profile (her fullest Q&A) states: "As city attorney, I would reform the criminal division and train attorneys to recognize cases where constitutional rights are violated and to not file those cases. My goal would be to abolish racial profiling in my office. This will also impact the LAPD as they will be disincentivized to arrest when they knew that we would not file those cases." Her platform page explicitly commits to "protecting our rights to protest and be free from unreasonable search and seizure" — directly naming LAPD-related issues. The LAist voter guide confirms: "Ashouri told LAist she's seen the need to protect vulnerable victims of crime and how overpolicing leads to racial profiling and the criminalization of poverty." The SPNA DTLA article summarizes her platform as: "Police Accountability: Emphasizes protecting the right to protest and freedom from unreasonable search and seizure." Her approach — training city prosecutors to refuse unconstitutional cases, abolishing racial profiling, declining to file cases where rights were violated (thereby disincentivizing improper LAPD arrests) — maps directly to value 1: the office investigates independently and works for the public, not for officials it is supposed to keep accountable. She would use prosecutorial declination as an accountability mechanism against LAPD, not to defend city employees.

## Topic 6: judicial-prosecution-priorities
**Topic ID:** `abb99d95-cbb1-4617-8f8b-f220ef6028ca`
**Title:** Prosecution Priorities

### Selected Value: 1

**Stance description for value 1:**
"Prosecution should be a last resort. Connecting people to treatment, housing, or job programs does more good than a criminal record."

### Reasoning:
Ashouri's prosecution-priority framing across all sources consistently treats prosecution as a last resort, with treatment, mediation, and community services as preferred interventions. Her platform page explicitly states "Housing over handcuffs" and advocates focusing "on alternatives to incarceration." Her Patch Q&A describes her dismissing cases in San Diego "where police did not properly investigate" and allowing "changes in sentencing for non-violent cases to allow for people to seek rehabilitation and schooling." She explicitly says she would "make a policy not to criminalize poverty" and would "also prioritize rehabilitation, and not treat substance abuse as a crime but a public health matter." Her platform explicitly calls for "prioritizing mediation and restorative justice" and restarting the Neighborhood Prosecutor program to "resolv[e] issues without punitive measures." The AOL/LA Times piece quotes her: "We need to focus on cases that are harming people" — implying a high bar for prosecution, not a blanket prosecution approach. She does acknowledge that vehicular crimes and gun cases need prosecution, and serious violent crimes should be prosecuted — but her systematic framework is that prosecution is the last resort after mediation, diversion, and treatment options are exhausted. This maps to value 1: prosecution as last resort; connecting people to treatment/housing/job programs does more good than a criminal record. The explicit "housing over handcuffs" framing and drug-as-public-health position support value 1 over value 2.
