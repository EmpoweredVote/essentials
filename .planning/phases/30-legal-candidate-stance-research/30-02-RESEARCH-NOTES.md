## Topic 1: judicial-prosecution-priorities
**Topic ID:** `abb99d95-cbb1-4617-8f8b-f220ef6028ca`
**Title:** Prosecution Priorities

### Selected Value: 4

**Stance description for value 4:**
"Prosecute all solid cases. Declination is the exception and needs a strong reason."

### Reasoning:
McKinney's prosecution framework is built around a broad set of cases that must be actively prosecuted: repeat offenders, retail theft and organized shoplifting, illegal firearms, street racing, open-air drug markets, and encampment-related quality-of-life crimes. His Patch Q&A states: "The focus of the Los Angeles City Attorney's Office must be on the crimes that are most directly impacting the public safety of our communities and the quality of life of our neighborhoods. That means prioritizing cases involving repeat offenders who drive quality-of-life crime through theft, vandalism, and destruction of property." He explicitly identifies five to six distinct offense categories requiring active prosecution. His "compassionate accountability" framing does include working with diversion programs and service providers, but this is described as a complement to — not a substitute for — prosecution: "we must work closely with service providers and diversion programs to help people access housing, treatment, and support. This is not about punishment. It is about restoring order to our public spaces while helping people move toward stability." Diversion is framed as helping people after enforcement contact, not as a reason to decline prosecution. This positions him at value 4: prosecute all solid cases, with declination requiring a strong reason. He does not reach value 5 because he explicitly acknowledges diversion's role and describes his approach as "not about punishment for its own sake."

## Topic 2: judicial-criminal-justice
**Topic ID:** `9db07b16-1076-4b7d-ad89-ebe7b51f4336`
**Title:** Criminal Justice Approach

### Selected Value: 4

**Stance description for value 4:**
"Making sure others think twice before doing the same thing."

### Reasoning:
McKinney consistently frames criminal justice in terms of deterrence and accountability to the community. The LAist voter guide reports: "McKinney said he believes in enforcing the law fairly and without fear or favor. 'As City Attorney, he will uphold the integrity of the justice system and city government.'" His campaign told LAist: "[McKinney] will focus on repeat offenders driving down quality-of-life crime, retail theft and organized shoplifting, illegal firearms, and dangerous activities such as street racing and open-air drug markets." The Patch Q&A states: "I've prosecuted cases ranging from misdemeanors to capital murder, conducting over 100 jury trials, including 40 murder cases, with a 97% conviction rate." His career framing centers on accountability and enforcement, with public safety restoration as the primary goal. He does include diversion and services language ("compassionate accountability"), but specifically describes these as means to "restor[e] order" rather than alternatives to enforcement. His platform's criminal justice vision — holding offenders accountable to protect communities, with deterrence as the mechanism — best matches value 4: making sure others think twice before doing the same thing. He does not reach value 5 (pure punishment) because he explicitly invokes dignity of individuals and acknowledges diversion programs.

## Topic 3: judicial-police-accountability
**Topic ID:** `7bad33eb-e93e-4d94-8822-97212d49bde5`
**Title:** Police Accountability

### Selected Value: 4

**Stance description for value 4:**
"Defend city employees vigorously. That's the job. Settlements invite more lawsuits."

### Reasoning:
McKinney's stated approach to police misconduct litigation and city liability is grounded in vigorous litigation management and cost reduction, not independent accountability. His Patch Q&A states: "As city attorney, I would implement an aggressive risk management strategy to reduce lawsuits and payouts, vigorously defend against frivolous claims, and settle meritorious cases early to avoid costly verdicts." He frames his litigation posture as knowing "which cases to fight and which to resolve" — a liability management lens, not a police accountability lens. His primary critique of the incumbent is about the LAPD data breach — a management and transparency failure — not about the office's failure to hold police accountable for misconduct. He is endorsed by the Los Angeles Police Protective League, the LAPD officer union, and former DAs with law-enforcement-aligned records. He makes no statement about independent investigation of police misconduct, civilian oversight support, or holding officers accountable. His platform does not mention police brutality, excessive force, or systemic LAPD accountability. The pattern — vigorous defense of city employees, settlement of meritorious claims to cut costs (not as accountability mechanism), police union endorsement — most closely matches value 4: defend city employees vigorously, as that is the job; settlements invite more lawsuits. He does not reach value 5 because he explicitly acknowledges settling meritorious cases rather than defending everything regardless of merit.

## Topic 4: judicial-access-to-justice
**Topic ID:** `9d45acaf-1ba4-4cb8-95e1-5ed985223b91`
**Title:** Access to Justice

### Selected Value: 3

**Stance description for value 3:**
"Reasonable standards that keep out frivolous cases without blocking legitimate ones."

### Reasoning:
McKinney's access-to-justice framing is balanced and pragmatic rather than access-maximizing or access-restricting. His LAist entry includes a notable quote: "McKinney said he respects constitutional protections that allow legal aid attorneys to challenge government actions. 'Limiting such advocacy through contract conditions or fear of lawsuits undermines legal services for the city's most vulnerable and under-served residents.'" This affirms he supports legal access for vulnerable residents and opposes barriers to legitimate legal advocacy. His Patch Q&A discusses protecting renters from unlawful evictions and protecting workers from wage theft — indicating he sees the legal system as a vehicle for these claims. At the same time, his litigation management approach ("vigorously defend against frivolous claims") and repeated emphasis on "reducing unnecessary lawsuits" reflect a concern for filtering out unmeritorious cases. His Issues page supports "balanced" enforcement of housing laws that "respect[s] the role responsible property owners play" alongside tenant protection — indicating moderate, case-by-case standards rather than either blanket openness (value 1-2) or high barriers (value 4-5). The combination of supporting legal aid access for the vulnerable while prioritizing efficient resolution and filtering frivolous claims best matches value 3: reasonable standards that keep out frivolous cases without blocking legitimate ones.

## Topic 5: judicial-government-deference
**Topic ID:** `e5e48f0e-8f3a-40e1-8080-889fea389603`
**Title:** Judicial & Prosecutorial Discretion

### Selected Value: 3

**Stance description for value 3:**
"Neither side automatically. Look at the facts and apply the law evenly."

### Reasoning:
For City Attorney candidates, this topic captures whether the candidate reflexively defends the city and its officials, or exercises independent judgment to settle meritorious claims even against the city's interest. McKinney explicitly commits to settling meritorious cases: Patch Q&A states "settle meritorious cases early to avoid costly verdicts. Effective litigation requires knowing which cases to fight and which to resolve, saving taxpayers money while ensuring accountability." This is neither reflexive defense (which would map to 4-5) nor a citizen-first stance (which would map to 1-2). He also describes "promot[ing] transparency in city contracting" and "ensur[ing] that taxpayer dollars are used responsibly" — framing the office's role as independent steward of public funds rather than as a defender of city officials' decisions. His criticism of the incumbent is about mismanagement, not about defending city actions; he frames himself as holding City Hall to account for mismanagement and LAPD data breaches. However, his "uphold the integrity of the justice system and city government" framing and institutional prosecutor background suggest a baseline respect for government decisions. His stated approach of applying the law "fairly and consistently" across cases best matches value 3: neither side automatically — look at the facts and apply the law evenly. Inferred from litigation management framing and accountability statements — no single direct quote addresses the citizen vs. government deference question explicitly.
