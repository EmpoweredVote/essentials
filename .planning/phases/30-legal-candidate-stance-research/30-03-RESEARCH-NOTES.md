## Topic 1: judicial-prosecution-priorities
**Topic ID:** `abb99d95-cbb1-4617-8f8b-f220ef6028ca`
**Title:** Prosecution Priorities

### Selected Value: 2

**Stance description for value 2:**
"Use diversion when it's available and makes sense. Reserve prosecution for when community safety actually requires it."

### Reasoning:
Roy's prosecution framework centers on diversion as the primary response to nonviolent misdemeanors, with prosecution reserved for cases that genuinely require it. In her Patch Q&A (the most detailed direct source), she writes: "Diversion is a tailored, court-supervised program that connects people with the services they need to address that cause, such as behavioral health treatment, addiction support and counseling. Data has shown that when nonviolent misdemeanors are addressed with diversion, rather than incarceration, people are dramatically less likely to reoffend." She explicitly states her philosophy as "develop tools like diversion that are shown to work, collaborate with communities to develop sustainable, neighborhood-centered solutions, and work to permanently break cycles of criminalization." The AOL/LA Times piece quotes her directly: "Roy, 34, has promised to place a heavy emphasis on the legal process known as diversion… In cases involving nonviolent crimes, diversion is more likely than jail to keep people from becoming repeat offenders. 'It makes not only the person whole, but the community safer.'" At the same time, Roy does not call for abandoning prosecution entirely — LAist reports she would "use the office's misdemeanor authority to prosecute ICE agents," and her Patch Q&A notes "That does not mean freezing misdemeanor prosecution — for example, we would vigorously prosecute rogue ICE agents — it means right-sizing and tailoring our approach." This is precisely value 2: use diversion when available and appropriate, reserve prosecution for when community safety actually requires it. She does not map to value 1 (prosecution as last resort / connecting to programs as better than criminal record) because she explicitly maintains active prosecution for cases where it serves community safety.

## Topic 2: judicial-criminal-justice
**Topic ID:** `9db07b16-1076-4b7d-ad89-ebe7b51f4336`
**Title:** Criminal Justice Approach

### Selected Value: 2

**Stance description for value 2:**
"Giving the person a fair chance to make things right — through treatment, community service, or restitution."

### Reasoning:
Roy's criminal justice philosophy is organized around breaking cycles of criminalization through evidence-based alternatives to incarceration. Her Patch Q&A states: "I want to pursue strategies that actually make our communities safer — one key goal is reducing recidivism, or the rate of reoffending. When we structure a public safety approach that breaks cycles of criminalization, we are preventing crime and making everyone safer." She explicitly describes diversion as connecting people to "behavioral health treatment, addiction support and counseling" — treatment and services as the mechanism for making people and communities safer. Her LAist candidate survey states: "I would work to restore and scale up diversion programs, so we can use all of the tools in our toolbox to address public safety." Her campaign website frames the goal as "strategies that break cycles of criminalization, and make everyone in our communities safer." The LA Forward endorsement (summarizing her stated positions) describes her as "pursuing smart public safety by prioritizing strategies that actually make communities safer — diversion, prevention, accountability — rather than reflexive criminalization that feeds mass incarceration." This maps to value 2: giving the person a fair chance to make things right through treatment, community service, or restitution. She is not at value 1 (pure rehabilitation focus) because she explicitly maintains prosecution for cases requiring it and does not frame the goal as purely helping the individual — she consistently frames outcomes in terms of community safety ("making everyone safer").

## Topic 3: judicial-police-accountability
**Topic ID:** `7bad33eb-e93e-4d94-8822-97212d49bde5`
**Title:** Police Accountability

### Selected Value: 2

**Stance description for value 2:**
"Settle valid claims quickly and pursue real accountability. Defending misconduct wastes money and public trust."

### Reasoning:
Roy's approach to police misconduct liability sits between independent investigation (value 1) and routine defense (values 3–5). The SPNA DTLA article identifies as a key platform element: "Critical of Incumbent: Her platform argues the current administration has spent too much on 'liability payouts' and has been overly aggressive in suing journalists and protesters." Her Patch Q&A states regarding legal liability: "For more long-term solutions, I'd work to bring departments in compliance with the law so that we prevent lawsuits before they occur" — framing LAPD compliance as a priority for reducing costs. The AOL/LA Times piece quotes her: "[Roy] vowed to put a particular focus on the Los Angeles Police Department, making sure it follows through on the recommendations drafted in the wake of costly litigation." This "follow through on recommendations" framing reflects accountability through compliance and prevention, not reflexive defense. Her proposed "liability audit" by City Controller Kenneth Mejia (AOL piece) would scrutinize the office's existing settlement and defense practices, including police-related cases. The LA Forward endorsement describes Roy as committed to "holding police accountable" and contrasts her with Feldstein Soto's posture of "defending city departments against lawsuits over police misconduct." Roy's position — settle valid claims, make LAPD follow through on recommendations from costly litigation, conduct audit of existing practices — maps to value 2: settle valid claims quickly and pursue real accountability. She does not reach value 1 (independent investigation against officials) because she does not explicitly frame the office as an independent police accountability body; her emphasis is on smart liability management that produces accountability as a byproduct.

## Topic 4: judicial-access-to-justice
**Topic ID:** `9d45acaf-1ba4-4cb8-95e1-5ed985223b91`
**Title:** Access to Justice

### Selected Value: 1

**Stance description for value 1:**
"Easy. Courts exist for everyone — not just people with expensive lawyers. Low barriers mean more access to justice."

### Reasoning:
Roy's entire campaign vision is built on eliminating barriers to legal access for workers, tenants, immigrants, and consumers who currently lack it. Her core platform commitment — stated consistently across all sources — is to make the City Attorney's Office "the largest public interest law firm in Los Angeles" (LAist, Patch, marissaroy.com). Her specific programmatic proposals are all access-maximizing: a dedicated "Tenants' Rights Team" to fight abusive landlords (SPNA), expanded wage theft investigation staffing, and using the office to pursue "workers, tenants, climate, civil rights" as top priorities (LAist). Her Patch Q&A describes bringing wage theft cases that "put thousands of dollars back in workers' pockets" — demonstrating the office as a legal vehicle for those who could not otherwise afford representation against corporate defendants. Her campaign website explicitly frames the mission as "protecting workers from wage theft, renters from illegal evictions, consumers from fraud" — using government legal resources to provide access to justice for people who cannot access it individually. Her career background — staff attorney at the Public Rights Project, consumer protection work at the California AG's office — embodies the value 1 framing: courts and legal resources exist for everyone, not just people with expensive lawyers. Inferred from overall career pattern and platform framing — no single explicit quote addressing court access barriers, but the consistent pattern is unambiguous: use public legal resources to provide access for those who lack it.

## Topic 5: judicial-government-deference
**Topic ID:** `e5e48f0e-8f3a-40e1-8080-889fea389603`
**Title:** Judicial & Prosecutorial Discretion

### Selected Value: 1

**Stance description for value 1:**
"The citizen, almost always. Government has lawyers, money, and power. Regular people need courts to level the playing field."

### Reasoning:
For City Attorney candidates, this topic captures whether the candidate sees the role as reflexively defending city decisions and employees, or exercising independent judgment to protect residents against city and corporate overreach. Roy explicitly frames the office's primary obligation as being to the public — workers, tenants, consumers — against both corporate and city interests. Her campaign website states: "As City Attorney, I'll restore integrity to the Office to make sure our City Attorney serves the people, not the powerful." Her LAist candidate survey describes her mission as "the most significant public interest law office in Los Angeles" to prioritize "workers, tenants, climate, civil rights." The LA Forward endorsement quotes her commitment to "holding abusive police accountable" and contrasts her with the incumbent's posture of defending "city departments against lawsuits over police misconduct." Her proposed liability audit and LAPD compliance enforcement — having City Controller Kenneth Mejia audit the office's settlement practices — signals that Roy sees independent review of city decisions (including city defense choices) as part of her mandate. Her entire career has been spent representing workers, tenants, and consumers against institutions (corporate and government) that have legal resources they lack. This places her squarely at value 1: the citizen, almost always; government has lawyers, money, and power; regular people need courts to level the playing field. Inferred from overall platform and career framing — no single quote explicitly addresses the "citizen vs. government" deference question, but the consistent direction across all sources is unambiguous.
