# Proposed Compass Questions — Local Scope
**Prepared:** 2026-05-03
**For review by:** Chris Andrews
**Status:** Draft — not yet implemented

---

## The Problem This Solves

The Empowered Vote compass currently asks politicians about Ukraine, tariffs, Medicare, and same-sex marriage. A city council member in Plano, Texas or a mayoral candidate in Los Angeles may have opinions on all of those — but those topics won't determine how your street gets cleaned, whether your neighborhood gets rezoned for apartments, or how the city responds when someone is sleeping in front of your business.

**The goal:** A compass that is actually fillable at the local level — built from questions that local candidates genuinely disagree on, that the office controls directly, and that matter to residents regardless of what city they live in.

**Design criteria for every question:**
- The city council or mayor must directly control the policy outcome
- Candidates take genuinely different positions on it — not just rhetorical variation
- Answers are verifiable from public record (voter guides, Q&As, campaign sites, votes)
- The question works across all U.S. regions and city sizes

## Input: Validation from an LA City Councilman

An ex-LA city councilman who now moderates a nonpartisan mayoral debate podcast identified the following as the issues candidates actually argue over. The table below shows how each maps to a proposed compass question.

| His topic | Maps to |
|---|---|
| Homelessness | Proposed: Homelessness Response ✓ |
| Affordable Housing | Existing compass topic ✓ |
| Immigration Raids | Proposed: Local Immigration Enforcement (new) |
| Crime | Proposed: Public Safety Approach ✓ |
| Jobs / Economy (film industry) | Proposed: Economic Development Incentives ✓ |
| Traffic | Proposed: Transportation Priorities ✓ |
| Trash, cleanliness | Proposed: City Sanitation and Cleanliness (new) |
| Affordability | Proposed: Rent Regulation (replaces Cost of Living) |
| Quality of parks and beaches | Folds into Environmental Protection question |
| Quality of public schools | See note below |

**Note on schools:** In most U.S. cities — including LA — school districts are governed by a separately elected school board. The mayor has limited direct control. Including it as a compass question would measure advocacy preferences, not actual governing power. Held for now.

## Proposed Questions

---

### 1. Residential Zoning

> *Distinct from the existing Housing topic, which addresses affordability programs and financial assistance. This is about who decides what gets built where.*

**Question:** "What should guide decisions about housing density and neighborhood character in your city?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Protect existing neighborhood character strictly; require community votes before any rezoning |
| 2 | Allow modest density increases (duplexes, accessory units) with strong design review and neighborhood input |
| 3 | Allow multifamily and mixed-use near commercial corridors while protecting most residential zones |
| 4 | Upzone broadly to allow multifamily by right; streamline approvals and reduce parking requirements |
| 5 | Eliminate single-family-only zoning; allow any housing type on any lot citywide |

**Why it differentiates:** Minneapolis eliminated single-family zoning. Houston has no zoning at all. Anti-apartment incumbents vs. pro-density challengers is one of the most common local campaign fault lines in the country.

---

### 2. Growth and Development Pace

> *Every growing city faces the question of how fast to expand. Shrinking cities face the inverse — whether to incentivize new development to reverse decline.*

**Question:** "How should your city manage population growth and new development?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Impose growth limits; require voter approval for major annexations or large-scale developments |
| 2 | Allow growth only where existing infrastructure can support it; slow approvals until capacity catches up |
| 3 | Plan proactively — invest in infrastructure ahead of growth to support responsible expansion |
| 4 | Streamline permitting, reduce fees, and actively recruit development to grow the city's tax base |
| 5 | Remove regulatory barriers to development entirely; let market demand determine growth pace |

---

### 3. Public Safety Approach

> *City councils set police budgets directly. This is the sharpest national fault line at the local level and shows no sign of fading.*

**Question:** "How should your city fund and operate public safety services?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Redirect a significant portion of the police budget to social services, mental health, and community programs |
| 2 | Maintain current police staffing but shift non-violent calls to unarmed mental health co-responders |
| 3 | Keep current public safety funding while adding crisis response teams for mental health and addiction calls |
| 4 | Increase police staffing, equipment, and pay to improve response times and deter crime |
| 5 | Make expanding the police budget the top city spending priority over other municipal services |

---

### 4. Homelessness Response

> *Different from the existing Housing topic, which addresses supply and affordability programs. This question is specifically about how the city responds to people living unsheltered — a top-three issue in city elections across all regions.*

**Question:** "What should be your city's primary strategy for addressing homelessness?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Housing-first: provide permanent supportive housing with no preconditions; avoid criminalization entirely |
| 2 | Expand shelter capacity and services as the primary strategy; use enforcement only after services are offered |
| 3 | Invest in outreach, shelter, and mental health services while enforcing reasonable public space rules |
| 4 | Enforce anti-camping ordinances as the primary tool while maintaining basic outreach programs |
| 5 | Prioritize strict enforcement of trespassing and camping bans; minimize city spending on homeless services |

---

### 5. Economic Development Incentives

> *City councils vote directly on tax abatements, tax increment reinvestment zones, and subsidies for employers. The "film industry" framing from the LA councilman is a local version of a universal question about whether cities should compete for major employers.*

**Question:** "How should your city attract businesses and support economic development?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | No corporate tax incentives; invest in public services and infrastructure to attract business organically |
| 2 | Small business support and local entrepreneur programs only; avoid large corporate subsidies |
| 3 | Targeted incentives for specific industries with community benefit agreements and job quality requirements |
| 4 | Compete actively for major employers with significant tax abatements and infrastructure investment |
| 5 | Offer maximum incentives to attract any large employer; economic growth is the top city priority |

---

### 6. Transportation Priorities

> *City councils control street design standards, parking minimums, and whether to fund transit connections. This divides candidates in every growing area.*

**Question:** "Where should your city focus its transportation investment?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Prioritize pedestrian infrastructure, cycling networks, and public transit; reduce parking requirements citywide |
| 2 | Invest equally in roads and multimodal options; require bike lanes and sidewalks on all new road projects |
| 3 | Maintain roads while selectively adding transit connections and pedestrian improvements where density supports it |
| 4 | Focus on road capacity and traffic flow; transportation investment should serve the majority who drive |
| 5 | Prioritize highway access and abundant free parking as the foundation of local transportation policy |

---

### 7. Environmental Protection vs. Development

> *Cities set their own tree ordinances, floodplain development rules, impervious cover limits, and park acquisition policies — independent of state and federal environmental law.*

**Question:** "How should your city balance new development with environmental preservation?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Require significant green space, tree preservation, and environmental review before approving any development |
| 2 | Protect existing parks and tree canopy strictly; require developers to fully offset any environmental impact |
| 3 | Apply consistent environmental standards while giving developers reasonable flexibility on implementation |
| 4 | Allow developers to pay fees in lieu of on-site preservation; prioritize economic activity over green space |
| 5 | Remove local environmental restrictions beyond what state and federal law requires |

---

### 8. Rent Regulation

> *This replaces "Cost of Living" as a proposed topic. Cost of living is a goal everyone shares; rent regulation is the policy mechanism where candidates genuinely disagree. Applies anywhere housing costs are debated — NYC, LA, Austin, smaller cities alike.*

**Question:** "What role should your city play in regulating rents and protecting tenants?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Expand rent control to all rental units with strong tenant protections and just-cause eviction requirements |
| 2 | Strengthen existing rent stabilization and extend coverage to more units |
| 3 | Maintain current tenant protections while allowing market rents for new construction |
| 4 | Limit rent regulations to subsidized units; allow market rents broadly |
| 5 | Oppose rent control entirely; rents should be set by the market without government intervention |

---

### 9. Local Immigration Enforcement

> *Mayors and city councils control whether local police cooperate with federal immigration authorities. This is not immigration policy — it is a question about how city law enforcement resources are used, and it is directly within local control.*

**Question:** "How should your city's police department relate to federal immigration enforcement?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Refuse all ICE detainers; prohibit city employees from sharing immigration status information with federal agencies |
| 2 | Comply only with court-ordered detainers; protect undocumented crime victims and witnesses from referral |
| 3 | Follow federal law as required but do not use city resources for proactive immigration enforcement |
| 4 | Honor ICE detainers and share information proactively when federal agencies request it |
| 5 | Direct city police to actively assist with immigration enforcement and support federal detention operations |

---

### 10. City Sanitation and Cleanliness

> *From the LA councilman's list. Bureau of Sanitation, street cleaning frequency, illegal dumping enforcement, and who bears responsibility for cleanliness are all city-controlled. The real debate is whether dirty streets are a services failure or an accountability failure.*

**Question:** "How should your city approach street cleanliness and sanitation?"

| Value | Stance |
|---|---|
| 1 | Significantly expand sanitation staffing, cleaning frequency, and free community disposal access; treat poor conditions as a services failure |
| 2 | Increase sanitation crews and prioritize historically underserved neighborhoods to equalize cleanliness citywide |
| 3 | Maintain current sanitation services while enforcing anti-dumping laws for businesses and large property owners |
| 4 | Rely primarily on enforcement of anti-littering and property maintenance laws; hold residents and businesses responsible |
| 5 | Privatize sanitation services and require residents and businesses to contract for cleanup directly |

## Topics Considered and Set Aside

| Topic | Reason held |
|---|---|
| City Services Quality (general) | "Do you want good services?" produces no differentiation — every candidate says yes. The real debates are captured by individual questions above. |
| Cost of Living (general) | Same problem — a goal, not a policy. Replaced by Rent Regulation, which is where the actual mechanism debate lives. |
| Short-term rentals (Airbnb/VRBO) | Real disagreement, but only salient in tourist towns and high-demand metros |
| City services privatization (general) | Rarely a front-line campaign issue; mostly plays out in budget hearings |
| School quality | School districts are separate elected bodies in most U.S. cities; limited direct mayoral control |
| 2020 Election | Design question pending — may not belong in the Compass widget; may need its own accountability feature with evidence markers |

## Appendix: Current Active Compass Topics and Stances

The following are the 26 active topics in the Empowered Vote compass. Topics marked **[LOCAL]** are directly applicable to city-level races. Topics marked **[FEDERAL]** address policy that is outside local government's jurisdiction. Topics marked **[STATE]** are primarily state-level. Topics marked **[LOCAL/STATE]** apply at both levels.

Note: Six earlier topic versions have been retired (Affordable Housing and Homelessness, AI Regulation, Deportation of Immigrants, Healthcare Access and Affordability, Immigration Policy, Taxation and Government Spending). All politician answer data from those topics has been migrated to the active versions below.
